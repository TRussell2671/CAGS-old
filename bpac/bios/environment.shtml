Environment Panel

Environment Panel Chair


Mr. J. M. Mauskar
Advisor to the Director
Observer Research Foundation

Shri Jayant Moreshver Mauskar is a former Indian Administrative Service officer of 1977 batch of Assam & Meghalaya cadre and retired as Special Secretary to Government of India in the Ministry of Environment & Forest. He is a B.Sc. from Punjab University, Chandigarh, M.Sc. from University of Pune and did research in Experimental Solid State, Physics at IIT, Kanpur from 1967 to 1977. During 35 years as a Public Servant, he has had “hands on” experience of International Trade, Investment Promotion, Overseas Investments, International Contracts, Dynamics of Petroleum Sector and Environmental Issues, especially Climate Change. In addition to the judicial experience of four years during his field postings in the North Eastern State of Meghalaya, he has been associated with regulatory work for more than 15 years in three Central Ministries, namely Commerce, Petroleum & Natural Gas and Environment & Forests. He also acted as Chairman, Central Pollution Control Board for two and half years. He was a Government Nominee on the Board of Directors for 5 years on key Central Public Sector Petroleum Enterprises, namely ONGC. After retirement, he continued to be associated till June 2013 with climate change negotiations and was a member of three important Central Government Committees. Additionally, as an Advisor to the Director in the Observer Research Foundation he continues to pursue his keen interest in environmental and trade matters and their impact on India’s international relations. He is Permanent Invitee to the Governing Council of the Indian Institute of Tropical Meteorology, Pune, known globally for its Climate and Monsoon research.


Environment Panel Commentator


Dr. Jayanta Bandyopadhyay
Professor
Jawaharlal Nehru University, New Delhi

Jayanta Bandyopadhyay was born in Calcutta in 1947. After completing his doctorate in engineering from the Indian Institute of Technology Kanpur, he turned his professional attention to the inter-disciplinary area at the complex interface between the natural environment and human economic activities. The transformation was facilitated by his close interaction with the forest movement in the Indian Himalaya called Chipko. He has been a consistent researcher on various critical policy issues related to environment in Asia, especially the rivers originating in the Himalaya. His research in the past 25 years has been guided by the objective of generating trans-disciplinary public interest knowledge on critical issues related to the natural environment, and in particular, the hydrosphere. His research formed the basis of important policy changes and court orders on Eucalyptus in social forestry, limestone mining in the Doon Vallya, and limitations of traditional engineering in designing of large water projects on the Himalayan rivers, etc. He was a Coordinating Lead Author in the global report of the Millennium Ecosystem Assessment and an expert reviewer for the Inter-governmental Panel on Climate Change in the area of climate change and water. He has been the President of the Indian Society for Ecological Economics (2006-8) and a Fellow of the India-China Institute in The New School, New York. He is also an adviser to the Water Diplomacy Program at Tufts University, Medford, USA. His recent book published by Bloomsbury entitled Environmental Governance has created an important impact in South Asia on how we need to look at nature.


Environment Panel Experts


Dr. Kelly Alley
Professor of Anthropology
Auburn University

Kelly Alley is Professor of Anthropology at Auburn University. She received her B.A. from Cornell University and her Ph.D. from the University of Wisconsin-Madison. She has carried out research in northern India for over 25 years, focusing on public culture and environmental issues. Her book, "On the Banks of the Ganga: When Wastewater Meets a Sacred River" (University of Michigan Press, 2002), explores Hindu interpretations of the sacred river Ganga in light of current environmental problems. She is also the author of book chapters and articles on religion, activism, politics, energy, policy and law related to rivers in India and she has written about environmental movements and environmental justice in the US. From 2001 to 2004 she worked with M C Mehta on a project that facilitated professional exchanges between environmental lawyers, scientists and NGOs to address river pollution problems in both countries. From 2005 to 2007, she worked with Julian Crandall Hollick and an interdisciplinary science team to create a radio series on the river Ganga for National Public Radio in the US. She is past-present of the Anthropology and Environment section of the American Anthropological Association. She is currently studying water governance in the Ganges-Brahmaputra-Meghna basin and with colleagues and students just produced an interactive website on hydropower across the Himalayas.

Dr. Rohan D’Souza
Assistant Professor
Centre for Studies in Science Policy at Jawaharlal Nehru University, New Delhi

Dr. Rohan D’Souza is Assistant Professor at the Centre for Studies in Science Policy (Jawaharlal Nehru University, New Delhi) and will be Associate Professor (April, 2015) at the Graduate School of Asian and African Area Studies at Kyoto University (Japan). He is the author of Drowned and Dammed: Colonial Capitalism and Flood control in Eastern India (2006). His edited books include The British Empire and the Natural World: Environmental Encounters in South Asia (2011) and Environment, Technology and Development: Critical and Subversive Essays (2012). His interests and research publications cover themes in environmental history, conservation, ecological politics, sustainable development and modern technology.


Dr. Doug Hill
Professor
University of Otago

Doug Hill completed a Bachelor of Arts (Hons) from the Australian National University and a PhD from Curtin University. He joined the University of Otago in 2006. Prior to this he was a Research Fellow, University of Wollongong for two years. Most of his work is concerned with South Asia, particularly India (with a focus on the state of West Bengal), although he also has research interests in Nepal, Pakistan and Bangladesh. His research has focused on four main areas: Rural Development, Environmental Management and Food Security; Transboundary Water Resources; Ports and Migrant Labour and; Indian Cities and Socio-Spatial Changes associated with the 'new' India. His current research projects include:

  • Geopolitics, Transboundary Water Resources and civil society in South Asia
  • The port sector and labour relations in Bangladesh, India and Malaysia
  • The transformation of urban space in Kolkata.
