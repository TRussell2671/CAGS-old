Russia's Growing Involvement in Syria

On December 2, 2015, the Center on American and Global Security held their second “Securi-Tea” of the semester, titled: “Russia’s Growing Involvement in Syria.” Our presenters for this event were Professor Dina Spechler from Political Science, and Professor Kevin Martin from Near Eastern Languages and Cultures at Indiana University, Bloomington.

Professor Martin focused his discussion on the historical involvement of Russia with Syria, which stemmed from the fall of the Ottoman Empire. During this period of time, Russia looked for ways to get out of isolation and develop friendships with countries near the Mediterranean. The newly designated Syria did not want to align themselves with the United States, and a Russian alliance appealed to their weak state. According to the agreement, Russia would provide weapons to Syria in return for Syrian materials. Furthermore, Russia had the opportunity to develop air force bases and increase their military presence in the country. Professor Martin sees consistency in the current situation. Syria still does not want a U.S. alliance, and Russia seeks to maintain presence in the country.

On the other hand, Professor Spechler sees a difference in the current Russian involvement in Syria. She indicated that in the past four months, Russia has increased its footprint in the country by expanding its Latakia Airbase, providing more sophisticated weaponry, and initiating a bombing campaign which has generally been directed at rebel groups. Professor Spechler noted that Russia’s involvement in Syria may be explained by two sets of objectives: regional and global. Her handout that she provided for the discussion is below:

Regional Objectives:
  • Pay back a long-standing political debt to the Assad family.
  • Keep a friendly Middle Eastern regime in power.
  • Maintain control of Russian naval and radar base at Tartus, established in early 1970’s.
  • Increase Russia’s ability to influence events in the Middle East.
  • Prevent the spread of Sunni Islamist extremism across the border of the Former Soviet Union and into Russia itself.
Global Objectives:
  • Demonstrate Russia’s loyalty to its friends.
  • Support the principle of the inviolability of state sovereignty.
  • Oppose perceived U.S. and NATO quest for world hegemony.
  • Demonstrate Russia’s newly enhanced force projection capability.
  • Give Russian military a chance to redeem itself after very poor showing in Georgia in 2008 and to practice in real combat conditions.
  • Test NATO unity, willingness to stand up to Russia when it uses force.
